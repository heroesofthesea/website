## What Is FIRST Tech Challenge?

FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC) is an international robotics competition for students in grades 7–12. Each season, the game challenge is revealed in September, and teams have roughly five months to design, build, program, and test a robot capable of completing a new set of tasks.

No two seasons are the same. That's the whole point. The game is always unfamiliar, and the robot you built last year will not win you this year's competition. You have to start over. And we love that.

### The Reveal Moment

Every year, FTC teams around the world gather — online, in gyms, in garages — to watch the official game reveal livestream together. This year was no different.

When the season was announced, we had about thirty seconds of silence before everyone started talking at once. That's how we know it's a good season.

## Our Goals This Year

We set three categories of goals at the start of every season: **technical**, **collaborative**, and **outreach**.

### Technical Goals

On the technical side, we want to significantly improve our autonomous programming. Autonomous mode — the first thirty seconds of each match where the robot runs entirely on pre-written code — is where consistent teams separate themselves from lucky ones.

We're also investing in driver practice earlier in the season. Past years we've underestimated how much driving skill compounds over time.

### Collaborative Goals

Heroes of the Sea is a student-led team. That means we also have to navigate the social dynamics of working in a high-pressure environment with people who have different skills, different working styles, and different stakes.

This season we want to formalize how we handle design decisions — who has final say on what, and how we document the reasoning so we can revisit choices later.

### Outreach Goals

We believe robotics education should be accessible to more students, especially those who've never seen themselves as "tech people." This year we're planning at least two community events and continuing our work with younger students who are interested in STEM.
